1.1 What Forecast Is and Is Not

What is a forecast if not a guess? An educated guess, nonetheless. We guess when we don’t know exactly how things may turn up. We may guess, for example, whether it will rain tomorrow. Or whether the next card out of the deck will be higher or lower than the current card.

We guess events that come with a fair bit of uncertainty. Such would be events for which several outcomes, each with some chance of appearing, are possible. In other words, we guess random variables.

Making a claim about whether it will rain the next day is a guess because it may or may not rain. Rain is a random variable. By contrast, making a claim about whether the sun will rise the next day is hardly a guess. There is little prophecy associated with predicting the sunrise.

A guess that relies on knowledge is a forecast. Otherwise, it is merely a gamble.1 Consider card counting. A casino is a gambling house. Everyone is welcome so long as gambling is what they do. But when one attempts to ‘beat the house’ using knowledge that is accumulated by counting cards, the gamble becomes a forecast, and the gambler becomes persona non grata. We make a forecast if we guess rain for tomorrow because we observed clouds today. Here we account for our understanding of meteorological phenomena. We gamble if we make a guess of heads after the next flip of a coin because we observed tails after the current flip. We have not acquired any new knowledge by observing the outcome of the current flip that would be useful in predicting the outcome of the next flip.

The more we know about underlying factors that affect the outcome of a random variable, the more accurate is our forecast. A good forecast doesn’t need to be precise, however. It almost never is, only if by fluke. An imprecise forecast can be of value as well. That we were unable to exactly predict an event, tells us something about our understanding of the underlying processes. Such conjecture can be useful. Forecasting, even if inaccurate, can be useful.2 George Box’s ‘all models are wrong but some are useful’ is certainly suitable for the study of forecasting.
